Layer 02
Blockchain Infrastructure

The CBK technical guidance document for Kenya VASP licensing contains a critical infrastructure specification that has gone unnoticed in public commentary: approved stablecoin issuers must maintain a qualifying custodian relationship with either a CBK-licensed bank or an internationally recognised digital asset custodian. This specification means that every VASP-licensed stablecoin issuer in Kenya will require a direct institutional custody relationship — creating immediate demand for qualified custody infrastructure in Nairobi. There are currently zero CBK-licensed digital asset custodians. The first institution to obtain CBK digital asset custodian classification will become the mandatory infrastructure partner for every stablecoin VASP in Kenya — and by regulatory precedent, likely every EAC stablecoin VASP as the 2031 masterplan rolls out.

Tron’s USDT dominance in West African P2P continues at ~81% share. The three BCEAO consultation responses — Ripple, Circle, Yellow Card — notably do not include Tether. Tether’s absence from the formal consultation response process is the most significant non-event of this cycle. An entity that controls 81% of the market for a product category that a regulator is about to formally define — and that does not participate in shaping that definition — is ceding regulatory architecture to its competitors. If the BCEAO framework’s compliance requirements are written to Ripple/Circle specifications and Tether cannot or will not meet them, USDT could be operationally excluded from the licensed WAEMU layer. The informal USDT layer would persist — but the licensed layer would be RLUSD and USDC. This is the most important competitive dynamic in African blockchain infrastructure that no one is modelling.

Layer 05
DeFi Shadow Banking

Kenya’s CBK technical guidance document contains a specific DeFi provision with significant structural implications: applicants operating automated market maker protocols or liquidity pool mechanisms must disclose the smart contract architecture and submit to a CBK-approved technology audit. This is the first African regulatory document to formally acknowledge DeFi as a category requiring licensing consideration. It does not ban DeFi. It requires disclosure and audit. For DeFi protocols with African user bases, this creates a compliance path that did not previously exist — and a corresponding obligation. Protocols that ignore the disclosure requirement in Kenya face potential delisting from VASP-licensed exchanges that must comply with CBK reporting.

The BCEAO consultation paper’s DeFi provisions (hostile to DeFi by architecture, per Vol. 07 analysis) are being challenged in the formal responses. Yellow Card’s submission argues explicitly for a licensed DeFi category — regulated decentralised finance — that would allow permissioned DeFi protocols to operate within the framework. This argument was not in the original consultation paper. If it is incorporated into the final framework, it would create a new category of African DeFi infrastructure that is simultaneously licensed and decentralised. The practical effect: Yellow Card and similar operators could launch liquidity pools that formal economy WAEMU users can access legally. The informal economy continues on Tron. Two-tier DeFi — licensed and unlicensed — may be the permanent architecture of African DeFi.

Layer 06
Governance & Power

The CBK technical guidance’s tiered capital structure represents a deliberate governance choice: Kenya has decided to govern a competitive crypto market rather than a consolidated one. A $4M flat requirement would have produced 3–5 dominant licensed operators. The tiered structure at $155K–$925K will produce 20–40 licensed operators. Kenya has chosen fragmentation over consolidation at the licensing stage — a governance decision with long-term market structure implications. Fragmented licensed markets develop more innovation but less capital efficiency. Consolidated licensed markets develop less innovation but stronger individual operators. Kenya’s governance choice favours ecosystem development over champion creation.

Morocco’s Attijariwafa signal reveals a governance dynamic that runs parallel to the AMMC framework: the banking sector is making its own regulatory decision independent of the AMMC process. Attijariwafa is not waiting for AMMC to license operators before extending correspondent services — it is proactively signalling in advance of the first license, to position itself as the banking partner of choice for whoever receives it. The banking sector is effectively co-governing the Moroccan crypto market’s emergence alongside the regulator. This dual-governance structure — central regulator plus banking sector — is more stable than single-regulator governance because it has two independent actors with aligned but distinct interests in the framework’s success.

Layer 08
Network Effects

Kenya’s VASP licensing event will produce a specific network effects structure that ACIOS can now model precisely, thanks to the CBK technical guidance. The three-tier structure creates three parallel network effect races: (1) among stablecoin issuers competing for the most-trusted stablecoin position, (2) among exchanges competing for the most-liquid exchange position, and (3) among payment providers competing for broadest merchant acceptance. These three races will compound over time: the stablecoin issuer with most trust will be listed on the most exchanges; the exchange with most liquidity will attract the most payment providers; the payment provider with most merchants will create demand for the most-trusted stablecoin. The network effect flywheel in Kenya has three entry points but converges to the same outcome: one dominant stack. The entity that leads all three tiers, or controls the critical integration point between them, captures the compounding flywheel.

The BCEAO consultation response race has created a network effects structure at the regulatory level. The entity whose compliance architecture becomes the BCEAO’s reference implementation gains a regulatory network effect: every subsequent regulator in WAEMU interprets the rules through that entity’s implementation. This is a new network effect category — regulatory architecture network effects. They are more durable than market network effects because they are embedded in law rather than user behaviour. They are more important than market network effects because they define the rules all market participants must follow. Ripple and Circle are competing for this position in WAEMU. Yellow Card is competing for it at the market layer. All three are correct about what is at stake.
